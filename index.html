Project Overview

What We're Analyzing

The following is an analysis of the top 50 banks in Evident Insights’s AI index, their financial performance based on available data from Yahoo Finance and the Wall Street Journal, and supplemented with historical data from company profiles in Forbes.

Our Learning Goals

We aim to explore how the integration of artificial intelligence has affected the banking industry, both internally (development processes) and externally (customer perceptions and satisfaction).

Artificial Intelligence is Everywhere

Every major bank now runs AI in production, yet their implementations reveal fundamentally different strategies. Among the top 50 banks globally, AI deployment concentrates in five core categories: Customer Experience (CX) leads with the most use cases, followed by Productivity & Automation, Operations & Infrastructure, Capital Markets & Research, and Risk & Fraud detection. The US dominates AI adoption with nearly 20 documented use cases, while Europe and Canada show moderate integration. This geographic concentration raises a central question: what determines how banks choose to deploy AI?

The History of AI in Banking

Early Automation

The introduction of “AI” in banking was with automated processes during the 50s and 60s, such as ATMs. This was primarily internal, assisting bank employees in completing calculations and daily tasks with a new level of efficiency. While different from what we know as AI today, methods developed during this time, such as Bayesian Statistics, laid the groundwork for the AI models of today (Capitol Technology University, 2025, & Swaine-Simon, 2018).

The Advent of AI

The years between 1970 and 1989 were spent integrating more automated processes in the customer flow, such as electronic credit cards. Additionally, the 1980s saw the beginning of AI development as we know it, with “over two-thirds of Fortune 1000 companies [developing] at least one AI project.”(Durkin, 2003 in Swaine-Simon, 2019) These systems involved creating artificial neural networks to predict stock market movement to optimize investing strategies, as well as creating financial plans for clients based on their income level. While they were not reliably effective, this proved the value of AI in finance and showed promise for the years to come.

Digital Banking Arrives

The 1990s saw a growth in the use of AI for fraud detection and prevention by financial institutions. The implementation involved using AI to filter through transactions and financial behaviour to look for suspicious activity. One example of this is the FinCEN Artificial Intelligence System (FAIS), which has been used in the United States of America since 1993 (Swaine-Simon, 2018). Comparatively, the 2000s saw an enormous increase in internet usage and, as a result, online banking. This opened the door for the future of customer-facing AI integration.

AI at Scale

In today’s scope (particularly the past 5 years), the progression of AI integration in companies has surged, with a reported 78% of organizations now using AI in at least one function, up from 55% just a year ago (Njegic, 2025). Internally, generative AI is being used for fraud detection, software development, and financial prediction. Externally, this can be seen in online and mobile banking interfaces, as AI chatbots and “digital assistants” are used for customer service purposes (Weiss, 2023).

Performance of Banks with AI

Banks with higher AI maturity demonstrate stronger financial performance across three key metrics: return on equity, revenue growth, and operational efficiency. Top-ranked banks (AI Maturity Rank 1-5) show higher returns and efficiency compared to mid-tier and lower-tier adopters. The performance gap is most pronounced in profitability and efficiency rather than revenue growth, suggesting AI’s primary impact lies in operational optimization (doing more with existing resources, rather than simply expanding faster).

This pattern raises an important question on whether banks are high performers because they have invested in AI or if their existing strength enabled greater AI investment. Likely, it is both in a reinforcing cycle where financial position funds AI development, which strengthens competitive advantages through reduced operational friction and improved decision-making.

Different Types of AI Integration

How banks prioritized these newly tractable problems reveal distinct implementation philosophies. CX applications are the most prevalent with 13 use cases, addressing conversational AI (chatbots), personalized recommendations, and intelligent routing. These visible deployments carry risk in reputation, with failures directly impacting brand perception. Productivity and automation follows closely with 10 cases, streamlining internal processes such as document processing, workflow optimization, and automated reporting. These implementations offer lower differentiation but with it, lower risk exposure.

AI in Banking, Across the Globe

Regional patterns reflect distinct technical and regulatory systems that shape deployment feasibility. US banks concentrate on CX (5 cases), operating in markets with high digital adoption and permissive AI regulation. European banks distribute investment more evenly, constrained by General Data Protection Regulation requirements. Canadian banks emphasize CX and Productivity within smaller, more regulated markets.

APAC banks demonstrate notable emphasis on Operations and Capital Markets applications. Many bypass legacy infrastructure constraints entirely, focusing on cloud-native core systems where AI integration presents fewer architectural challenges. The technical debt constraining Western institutions exerts less influence on their deployment choices. These variations represent system-level constraints that determine which AI implementations are technically feasible, legally permissible, and economically rational within specific contexts.

Why is this important?

The geographic and categorical patterns in AI deployment reveal that banks face different technical and regulatory constraints, leading them to prioritize distinct use cases rather than following a universal guideline. Successful AI deployment in banking depends not on model superiority, but on strategic problem selection/prioritization within institutional constraints. Banks aligning implementation with their technical infrastructure, regulatory environment, and competitive position show measurable advantages.

These patterns carry implications beyond sector competition. Banks process trillions in transactions, determine credit access for billions, and influence global economic stability. The AI systems that deploy shape tangible outcomes at scale. Credit models trained on historical data risk perpetuating bias, fraud detection systems may disproportionately flag specific populations, or customer service AI may lack appropriate escalation and cause compliance exposure.

This environment provides a high-stakes case study in AI deployment when systems are complex and have significant consequences. The patterns observed here anticipate challenges every sector faces as AI transitions from experimental deployment to production infrastructure shaping millions of decisions daily.
