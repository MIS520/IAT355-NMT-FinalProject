Project Overview

What We're Analyzing

The following is an analysis of the top 50 banks in Evident Insights’s AI index, their financial performance based on available data from Yahoo Finance and the Wall Street Journal, and supplemented with historical data from company profiles in Forbes.

Our Learning Goals

We aim to explore how the integration of artificial intelligence has affected the banking industry, both internally (development processes) and externally (customer perceptions and satisfaction).

Artificial Intelligence is Everywhere

Major banks worldwide use AI, but with varied strategies. Globally, the top 50 banks focus AI deployment across five main areas: Customer Experience/CX (leading), Productivity & Automation, Operations & Infrastructure, Capital Markets & Research, and Risk & Fraud. The US leads significantly in AI adoption, contrasting with moderate integration in Europe and Canada. This geographic difference raises the core question: what drives banks' AI deployment choices?

The History of AI in Banking

Early Automation

The introduction of "AI" in banking was with automated processes during the 50s and 60s, such as ATMs. This was primarily internal, assisting bank employees in completing calculations and daily tasks with a new level of efficiency. While different from what we know as AI today, methods developed during this time, such as Bayesian Statistics, laid the groundwork for the AI models of today (Capitol Technology University, 2025, & Swaine-Simon, 2018).

The Advent of AI

The years between 1970 and 1989 were spent integrating more automated processes in the customer flow, such as electronic credit cards. Additionally, the 1980s saw the beginning of AI development as we know it, with "over two-thirds of Fortune 1000 companies [developing] at least one AI project."(Durkin, 2003 in Swaine-Simon, 2019) These systems involved creating artificial neural networks to predict stock market movement to optimize investing strategies, as well as creating financial plans for clients based on their income level. While they were not reliably effective, this proved the value of AI in finance and showed promise for the years to come.

Digital Banking Arrives

The 1990s saw a growth in the use of AI for fraud detection and prevention by financial institutions. The implementation involved using AI to filter through transactions and financial behaviour to look for suspicious activity. One example of this is the FinCEN Artificial Intelligence System (FAIS), which has been used in the United States of America since 1993 (Swaine-Simon, 2018). Comparatively, the 2000s saw an enormous increase in internet usage and, as a result, online banking. This opened the door for the future of customer-facing AI integration.

AI at Scale

In today's scope (particularly the past 5 years), the progression of AI integration in companies has surged, with a reported 78% of organizations now using AI in at least one function, up from 55% just a year ago (Njegic, 2025). Internally, generative AI is being used for fraud detection, software development, and financial prediction. Externally, this can be seen in online and mobile banking interfaces, as AI chatbots and "digital assistants" are used for customer service purposes (Weiss, 2023).

Performance of Banks with AI

Banks with higher AI maturity show stronger performance in return on equity (ROE), revenue growth, and operational efficiency. Top AI adopters outperform mid- and lower-tier banks, especially in profitability and efficiency, suggesting AI primarily optimizes operations rather than simply expanding revenue faster. This performance link likely stems from a reinforcing cycle: strong financial position enables AI investment, which further strengthens the competitive advantage through operational improvements and better decision-making.

Different Types of AI Integration

How banks prioritize these problems reveals distinct implementation philosophies. CX applications lead with 13 use cases spanning conversational AI, personalized recommendations, and intelligent routing, carrying reputational risk where failures directly impact brand perception. Productivity and automation follows with 10 cases covering document processing and workflow optimization, offering lower differentiation but lower risk exposure.

The internal-external distinction carries real technical weight. External systems face stricter regulatory scrutiny and explainability requirements, where a chatbot generating incorrect information erodes trust and a fraud detection system that cannot justify its decisions fails compliance. Organizations prioritizing CX accept this engineering complexity; those focusing on operations optimize for accuracy in controlled environments where failures carry limited external cost.

Customer Experience

AI-powered assistants and advisors help customers manage money, get instant answers, and receive personalized financial guidance.

Productivity and Automation

AI handles meeting summaries, internal Q&A, and content generation so employees can focus on higher priority work.

Risk and Fraud

Real-time AI systems detect suspicious transactions, flag anomalies, and strengthen cybersecurity across global networks.

Capital Markets & Research

AI accelerates how analysts query data, synthesize research, and draft complex financial documents.

Operations & Infrastructure

Modernizes bank tech stacks by automating code review, data security, and platform monitoring and maintenance.

AI in Banking, Across the Globe

Regional patterns reflect distinct technical and regulatory systems that shape deployment feasibility. US banks concentrate on CX (5 cases), operating in markets with high digital adoption and permissive AI regulation. European banks distribute investment more evenly, constrained by General Data Protection Regulation requirements. Canadian banks emphasize CX and Productivity within smaller, more regulated markets.

Why is this important?

Banks deploy AI based on their unique technical and regulatory constraints, prioritizing distinct use cases instead of following universal guidelines. Successful AI deployment hinges on strategically selecting problems aligning with institutional constraints (infrastructure, regulation, competitive position), not merely superior models.

These patterns are critical because banking AI influences global economic stability, credit access, and billions of transactions. AI systems can perpetuate bias (credit models), disproportionately flag specific groups (fraud detection), or create compliance exposure (customer service AI).

Banking offers a high-stakes case study for complex AI deployment with significant consequences, anticipating challenges all sectors face as AI moves from experimental to production infrastructure shaping countless daily decisions.
